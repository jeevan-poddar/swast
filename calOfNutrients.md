# BASELINE CALCULATION OF NUTRIENTS
Phase 1: Calculating Total Daily Energy (TDEE)

1. BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate): The calories burned if the user stayed in bed all day. Calculated using the Mifflin-St Jeor Equation (Age, Gender, Weight, Height).

2. TEF (Thermic Effect of Food): The energy used to digest food (usually 10% of total calories).

3. NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis): This is where your "Job Type" and "Commute" inputs live (Walking to class, standing as a teacher).

4. EAT (Exercise Activity Thermogenesis): This is the "Add Task" section (Gym, Cricket, Cycling).

The Formula:  
-- 
TDEE = BMR + NEAT + EAT + TEF


Phase 2: Distributing the Macronutrients

1. Protein (The "Repair" Nutrient)

- Desk Life $0.8\text{g}$ to $1.0\text{g}$ per kg of body weight.

- Mostly Standing (The Teacher / Pharmacist) $1.1\text{g}$ to $1.2\text{g}$ per kg of body weight.

- Constant Movement (The Nurse / Waiter) $1.3\text{g}$ to $1.5\text{g}$ per kg of body weight.

- Labor / Pro Athlete (The Construction Worker / Virat Kohli) $1.6\text{g}$ to $2.2\text{g}$ per kg of body weight.

2. Fats (The "Hormone" Nutrient) : 20% to 30% of total TDEE.

3. Carbohydrates (The "Fuel" Nutrient) 

$Total Calories - (Protein Calories + Fat Calories) = Carb Calories$

$4 Calories = 1g Carbs$

4. Fiber: The rule is 14g of fiber for every 1,000 calories eaten.

5. Water: The general recommendation is 3.7 liters (or about 13 cups) for men and 2.7 liters (or about 9 cups) for  women, but this can vary based on activity level and climate. A good rule of thumb is to drink half your body weight in ounces of water daily (e.g., if you weigh 150 lbs, aim for 75 ounces of water).

6. Iron: 18mg (Higher for women, ~8mg for men).
7. Calcium: 1,000mg – 1,200mg.
8. Vitamin D: 600-800 IU (Higher for older adults).
9. Sodium/Potassium: 2,300mg Sodium / 4,700mg Potassium.
10. Omega-3 Fatty Acids (DHA/EPA): 250-500mg combined daily. (Found in fatty fish, flaxseeds, walnuts).
11. Magnesium: 400-420mg for men, 310-320mg for women.
12. Vitamin B12: 2.4mcg for adults (Higher for pregnant or breastfeeding women).
13. Antioxidants (Vitamin E & Selenium): Vitamin E (15mg) and Selenium (55mcg) daily. (Found in nuts, seeds, spinach, and seafood).

we are considering the following nutrients for the calculation of the daily intake:
Protein, Carbohydrates, Fats, Fiber, Water, Iron, Calcium, Vitamin D, Sodium/Potassium, Omega-3 Fatty Acids (DHA/EPA), Magnesium, Vitamin B12, Antioxidants (Vitamin E & Selenium).


# GOAL CALCULATION OF NUTRIENTS



1kg == 7,700 calories
$$\text{Daily Adjustment} = \frac{X \times 7,700}{Y}$$

Where:
- $X$ = Desired weight change in kg (positive for gain, negative for loss).
- $Y$ = Time frame in weeks to achieve the goal.

Changes in Nutrients: 


The "Intensity" LogicAs the ratio of $X$ to $Y$ becomes more aggressive (e.g., losing a lot of weight in a very short time), the nutrient priorities shift to prevent health failure.

A. If $X/Y$ is High (Aggressive Goal)

Protein (Critical Increase): 

In a steep deficit (Weight Loss), the body tries to burn muscle. The system must spike Protein to $2.0\text{g}–2.5\text{g}/kg$ to protect the heart and muscles.

Fiber (High): Used as a "hunger suppressant.

"Micronutrients (Safety Net): In a steep deficit, the user is eating less food, meaning they are at high risk of missing your 13 nutrients. The system must prioritize Nutrient-Dense "Budget Kings" (like Amla, Spinach, and Eggs) to hit 100% Daily Value (DV) on fewer calories.

Sodium/Potassium: Essential to prevent fainting or "brain fog" during the rapid shift.

B. If $X/Y$ is Low (Slow/Healthy Goal)

Carbohydrates (Balance): The system keeps Carbs higher to fuel the user's Job Type (Desk/Labor) without stress.

Fats (Standard): Kept at 25% for hormonal health and skin/hair quality.

Omega-3 & B12: Focus shifts to long-term brain health and energy.

|---|---|---|---|
| Carbohydrates | "Primary Spike: Used as the main ""weight driver.""" | "Reduced: But kept enough to fuel the ""Job Type.""" | Balanced: Matches daily activity.| 
| Protein | "High: To ensure weight is muscle, not just fat." | "Very High: To prevent muscle loss during the deficit." |  "Moderate: Based on Job Type."
| Fats | Slight Increase: For easy calorie density. | Lowered: To the 20% safety floor. | Balanced: For hormonal health. |
